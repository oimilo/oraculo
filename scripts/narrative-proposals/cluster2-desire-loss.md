# Cluster 2: Desire, Memory & Loss
## Sources: Freud (Transience, Why War?), Proust, Sophocles

---

## SCENARIO 1: The Garden That Will Burn
**Escalation Level:** Light

**Scene Setup:**
You stand at the entrance to a garden in full bloom. Every flower is open, every color at its peak. But you can smell smoke on the wind -- by morning, nothing here will remain.

**The Choice:**
"You have one hour in this garden. Do you walk slowly, touching every petal... or do you turn around now, before you see what you'll lose?"

- **Choice A -- Enter the garden:** The visitor is willing to attach to what is already dying. They accept the wound that comes with beauty. This suggests a capacity for mourning, a willingness to let experience mark them.

- **Choice B -- Turn away:** The visitor protects themselves by preemptive withdrawal. They devalue what will be lost before losing it. "If I never had it, it can't be taken from me." Anticipatory grief disguised as wisdom.

**Source:** Freud, "On Transience" -- the walk with the melancholic poet who could not enjoy beauty because it would perish.

**Devolucao:** "You noticed the fire before it started. You always do. The question is -- what have you missed while watching for smoke?"

## SCENARIO 2: The Locked Room
**Escalation Level:** Medium

**Scene Setup:**
There's a room in your memory you haven't opened in years. You can feel it -- heavy, sealed, warm. Something is alive in there. It might be precious. It might be unbearable.

**The Choice:**
"The door is in front of you. Do you open it... or do you seal it tighter and keep walking?"

- **Choice A -- Open:** The visitor chooses knowledge over comfort. The Oedipal impulse -- the need to know, even when knowing destroys the ground you stand on. Some people cannot tolerate mystery, and mistaking that compulsion for bravery is its own blindness.

- **Choice B -- Seal and walk:** The visitor chooses continuity over rupture. Some doors, once opened, cannot be closed again. This can be a profound respect for what the psyche has sealed for good reason. Or a preference for numbness over the risk of feeling.

**Source:** Oedipus Rex; Proust's narrator hovering outside the doors of memory.

**Devolucao:** "Some people build their whole house around the room they won't enter. Yours has become the largest room."

## SCENARIO 3: The Voice That Calls Your Name
**Escalation Level:** Medium

**Scene Setup:**
In the dark, someone is calling your name. The voice is familiar -- someone you loved, or someone who loved you. You can't tell if they need you or if you need them. The voice is getting fainter.

**The Choice:**
"Do you follow the voice... or do you let it fade?"

- **Choice A -- Follow:** The visitor runs toward connection, toward the echo of a bond. This can be love. It can also be an inability to let go -- following them means walking backward. Are you going toward them, or away from yourself?

- **Choice B -- Let it fade:** The visitor allows loss to complete itself. The hardest form of mourning: letting the beloved voice dissolve without chasing it. Or practiced detachment that no longer costs anything -- and that costlessness is itself a loss.

**Source:** Proust -- love as projection; Orpheus myth; Freud's mourning as liberation of desire.

**Devolucao:** "You know how to let go. You're very good at it. When did letting go become easier than holding on?"

## SCENARIO 4: The Two Waters
**Escalation Level:** Deep

**Scene Setup:**
You arrive at a place where two rivers meet. One is warm and dark -- it carries everything you've lived, every scar, every tenderness. The other is clear and cold -- it erases. Step into it and you emerge clean, blank, new.

**The Choice:**
"Which water do you enter -- the one that remembers... or the one that forgets?"

- **Choice A -- Remembers:** The visitor chooses to carry their history. Identity is woven from scars as much as joys. Pain as something to be digested, not expelled. They would rather be wounded and whole than clean and empty.

- **Choice B -- Forgets:** The visitor longs for release. Not from life, but from the accumulated weight of having lived. The water that erases does not discriminate. It takes the tenderness with the scars. The visitor may not have reckoned with what they would lose along with the pain.

**Source:** Proust -- involuntary memory as the substance of identity; Freud "On Transience"; River Lethe.

**Devolucao:** "You want to be new. But the room you sealed earlier -- it would have gone into the clear water too. And part of you knows: that's why you chose it."

## SCENARIO 5: The Other One
**Escalation Level:** Deep

**Scene Setup:**
There is someone in your life -- maybe still, maybe gone -- whom you never fully reached. You came close. You touched the edge of who they were. But there was always a wall, or a river, or a silence between you.

**The Choice:**
"If you could cross that distance now -- knowing you might find that there was never anyone on the other side -- do you cross... or do you keep the image you have of them?"

- **Choice A -- Cross:** The visitor privileges truth over consolation. They would rather discover an absence than maintain a beautiful fiction. Someone who suspects that love built on projection is a lonelier form of solitude than actual solitude.

- **Choice B -- Keep the image:** The visitor reveals a sophisticated understanding of how love works. We never love the person; we love our construction. But there is also a fear that reality would be insufficient, that the other person, fully seen, would not sustain the weight projected onto them.

**Source:** Proust -- love as hallucination, Swann's obsession; Oedipus at Colonus.

## SCENARIO 6: The Last Offering
**Escalation Level:** Profound

**Scene Setup:**
Everything you have -- every memory, every love, every wound, every moment of beauty -- you can offer it all to the next person who enters this room. They will feel it. But you will leave lighter. Emptier. Freer.

**The Choice:**
"Do you leave something behind for the one who comes after you... or do you take it all with you?"

- **Choice A -- Leave behind:** The visitor chooses transmission over possession. Experience is not diminished by being shared. The deepest answer to transience: beauty is MORE because it can be given. The offering makes the thing eternal in the only way anything can -- by touching someone else.

- **Choice B -- Take it all:** The visitor claims their life as entirely their own. Dignity in refusal. But to take it all is to carry it all. The weight of a life fully retained, never released -- that weight has a name. It is called solitude. The aloneness of hands held so tightly they are no longer open.

**Source:** Freud "On Transience" -- transience increases value; "Why War?" -- Eros as binding force; Antigone -- honoring what is owed.

**Devolucao:** "You walked in, you carried it, and now you give it away. The Oracle sees someone who has understood: the only things that last are the things you release."
